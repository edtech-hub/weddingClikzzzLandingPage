Navneet & Kaur: A Sikh Anand Karaj at Gravesend Gurdwara

15 January 2026 ■ 6 min read ■ By Heston Photo
Home› Blog› Navneet & Kaur at Gravesend Gurdwara

The Gravesend Gurdwara is one of the oldest and most beautiful Sikh places of worship in the United Kingdom. When Navneet and Kaur chose it for their Anand Karaj, they were choosing a space that carries generations of devotion within its walls — and the photographs it yields carry that weight too.

The Morning of the Milni

We arrived before sunrise. The Gurdwara was already alive — volunteers arranging langar in the kitchens, family members greeting each other in the forecourt with embraces that hadn't been exchanged since the last wedding, last Diwali, last loss. These are the moments that never make a highlight reel but that couples always point to years later and say: that was the day.

The Milni — the formal greeting between the two families — took place as the first pale light came through the arched windows. We worked quietly and at a distance, using longer lenses to preserve the intimacy of the moment while ensuring no important frame was missed.

The Anand Karaj

Kaur entered the Darbar Sahib in a deep crimson salwar kameez embroidered in gold, her dupatta arranged over her head with the careful, practised hands of her mother and elder sister. The congregation fell quiet as she walked to where Navneet waited beside the Guru Granth Sahib Ji.

The four Lavaan — the circling of the holy scripture — are the heart of the Anand Karaj. Each circuit is accompanied by a shabad sung by the ragis, and with each pass, the couple's families leaned slightly closer, as if drawn in by something invisible. We captured each Lavan from a respectful position at the side of the hall, never obstructing the congregation's view or the sanctity of the ritual.

"Photography in a Gurdwara requires genuine respect for the space. Heston Photo understood that without us having to say a word." — Navneet's father

After the Ceremony

The reception was held at a local banqueting hall nearby. After the ceremony, we took Navneet and Kaur for a brief portrait session in the Gurdwara gardens — an unplanned half hour that yielded some of the most natural images of the entire day. Both were laughing, relieved, entirely themselves.

It is always these unscheduled moments — a quiet ten minutes between the ceremony and the reception — that produce the photographs couples treasure most.
